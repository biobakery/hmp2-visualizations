Your IBD Multi'omics microbiome

Welcome!

Thank you for participating in the IBD Multi'omics project. Here, you can explore the information that the project has collected so far about your gut microbes and those of the other project participants. This page provides a summary of your gut microbial information collected over the course of the study. It also contextualizes your gut in the larger population of all IBD Multi'omics participants. Your results contribute greatly to our understanding of Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, and we hope that the project will help to generate new treatments for these conditions.

The human gut microbiota is one of the most complex reservoirs of microbial diversity, hosting hundreds of bacterial species. Large groups of related species are called phyla, and only a few of these are common in the human microbiome: Firmicutes , Bacteroidetes , and Actinobacteria represent about 75% of the organisms within the gut.

Your microbial profile to date

This diagram represents the proportions of different groups (phyla) of microbes in your gut from to most recent samples you've provided. For security and privacy reasons, we're not allowed to label these samples with specific dates, so up to the 12 most recent samples are shown. This allows you to see how your own microbial community varies over time.


Your gut similarity compared to others

This diagram shows all stool samples collected from all participants in the IBD Multi'omics project. Each dot represents a single sample, and the distance between points indicates how dissimilar they are. The closer together two samples' points appear, the more similar their microbial communities are. Your samples as collected over time are marked in green, so you can see how your own gut microbiome changes over time and in relation to other participants' guts.


Your average microbial profile

This bar graph shows the average abundance of each major group (phylum) of microbes in your microbiome as compared to their averages over all other participants in the study. It's normal for everyone to have big differences in our microbiomes, both between different people and in our own guts from day to day, even in health.
